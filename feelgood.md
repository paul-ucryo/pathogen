# Patient's practice, Dr's coach

Doctors should not have patients. They should have students.

The word *practice* has been hiding in plain sight for so long we stopped hearing it. We made it charming — the reassuring humility of a profession that never stops learning. But practice implies you are still figuring it out, and you are figuring it out on someone.

The better model is the coach. The coach is not there to fix you. The coach is there to make you legible to yourself — to explain why you are in distress, what is happening, what it means. The expertise doesn't flow into you like a prescription. It becomes yours.

This changes what a good outcome looks like. It is not *the doctor solved it*. It is *the patient understands something they did not before*. One of those outcomes ends when you leave the office. The other one goes home with you.

Almost nothing in the current system incentivizes the second kind. The incentives run the other way — toward volume, toward intervention, toward the doctor remaining indispensable. A patient who understands their own body is, in some sense, a patient you are working yourself out of.

That is exactly what a good teacher does.
