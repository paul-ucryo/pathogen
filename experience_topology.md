# Experience Topology

*A proposed framework for thinking about cognition, experience, and coherence.*

## The Brain as Correlator

What if we've been looking at the brain the wrong way? We study neurons the way you might study turbulent flow inside a generator — fixating on the mechanics of individual components while missing the architecture of what the system actually does. You don't need to understand turbulence to understand power generation. You need to understand the inputs, the outputs, and how the energy is structured between them.

In this framing, the brain is a correlator. Neurons are phase shifters. The brain doesn't process information into categorized storage — it doesn't maintain separate regions for "visual" and "verbal" and "emotional." Those are content-type labels we project onto a system that may be organized by something else entirely: phase. What the brain might actually store is phase relationships, held in whatever topology is required for access. The storage structure would then be organized not by what something is about, but by what retrieval geometry is needed to reach it.

If this is right, then memory isn't a filing cabinet with labeled drawers. A memory is a phase relationship embedded in an access structure. Without the right topology, the memory isn't "forgotten" — the path to it simply doesn't exist.

## Frequency, Not Agency

The conventional view says the brain exists to keep you alive. But that smuggles in an agent — it assumes the brain knows about "you" and has a goal. What if we strip that out? There may be no homunculus. No processor receiving inputs and making decisions.

What there might be instead: a system that operates on frequency. It doesn't represent the world. It correlates with it. The patterns of correlation shift — phase transitions in a state space — and what we call experience could be understood as the geometry that constrains which transitions are available from any given state.

In this view, experience isn't input to the system. Experience *is* the shape of the state space. What you have lived through doesn't give you data. It reshapes which states are reachable from where you are now.

And "you" aren't a thing the brain serves. "You" are what it looks like from the inside when the system occupies a particular region of that state space.

## The Environment Defines the State Space

If the brain is implementation — hardware coupled to an information topology — then the interesting dynamics might not live in the neurons at all. They might live in the information structure itself.

The environment defines the state space. Information could form attractors not inside the brain but in the information topology. The brain navigates that structure, but the structure exists independently of the substrate running it. You could, in principle, couple different hardware to the same information topology and see the same dynamics, because the dynamics would be properties of the topology, not the correlator.

What would matter then is not what the brain does, but the state space and the binding potential for navigating it.

## Control Dynamics: Gain and Hysteresis

The way a system navigates an information topology might be usefully described through its control dynamics — specifically, the relationship between gain (how much the system amplifies signal) and hysteresis (how sticky the system's states are).

Consider three points on this spectrum:

**High gain, high hysteresis — the psychotic state.** Everything is amplified. Every signal matters intensely. And the system is sticky: once it enters an attractor basin, the walls are steep. Getting out becomes nearly impossible. The information structure has deep wells that capture the system and hold it.

**High gain, low hysteresis — the infant.** Everything is amplified here too. Everything is salient. But nothing sticks. The attractor basins are shallow, spread everywhere. The system moves freely across the entire state space. This might be why babies appear to be in a state of constant overwhelm and wonder simultaneously — informationally, they could be. The binding potential is low, so nothing locks in, and the system flows.

**Healthy adult cognition would sit somewhere in between** — moderate gain with tuned hysteresis. Attractors deep enough for stability, shallow enough for transition. The system can settle when it needs to and move when it needs to.

Importantly, these wouldn't be descriptions of brain states. They would be descriptions of how a system relates to an information topology. The PID structure of the dynamics — not the circuits that produce them.

## Experience Exists in Change

Here is perhaps the most important move in this framework: experience *is* change. Not "experience requires change" or "experience is caused by change." Change would be the medium in which experience exists. If nothing is changing, there is no experience. Stasis isn't discomfort. Stasis is nonexistence.

If there is only change, then within it there seem to be two primitives:

**Resolution dissolving** — structure breaking apart, the current attractor losing coherence. This is what we call discomfort.

**Resolution forming** — new structure binding, a new attractor basin taking shape. This is what we call comfort.

Both are always happening. You can't form new resolution without dissolving old resolution. The question is never "is there discomfort or comfort?" The question is: what is the path? Which primitive dominates the trajectory?

Cross-country skiing is constant dissolution and formation — muscles straining and releasing, balance lost and regained, breath spent and recovered. But the path is dominated by formation. The discomfort is embedded in a trajectory toward new resolution. You can feel it going somewhere.

An avalanche is the same two primitives — structure breaking and reforming. But the path is dominated by dissolution. Structure collapses without trajectory. The breaking leads nowhere.

The discomfort isn't the enemy of the comfort. It's part of it. The path tells you which dominates the experience.

## What "Good" and "Suffering" Might Actually Be

In this framework, good and suffering aren't moral categories. They aren't subjective judgments layered on top of neutral events. They could be properties of the dynamics themselves.

Good would be when the system navigates the state space fluidly — dissolution serving formation, attractors you can enter and leave, gain matched to the landscape, the path dominated by resolving.

Suffering would be when the dynamics break. Stuck in an attractor you can't exit. Gain so high everything overwhelms. Hysteresis so deep you can't transition. Or dissolution without trajectory — structure breaking with no path toward new resolution.

This might reframe familiar states:

Depression isn't sadness — it's the change dynamics approaching zero. The system nearing stasis. The person isn't feeling bad so much as approaching nonexistence, ceasing to have experience at all.

Anxiety could be high gain with no stable attractor. Everything amplified, nowhere to settle.

Trauma might be a region of state space with walls so steep the system keeps falling back in. The geometry itself traps.

If this holds, these aren't metaphors. They're closer to what these states actually *are* when you understand experience as dynamics in an information topology.

## The Yoga of World Peace

Now consider scaling this up.

In a coherent system — whether a body, a community, or a civilization — every node contributes to the nodes around it. The resolution dynamics flow across the whole structure. This is arguably what yoga actually is. Not relaxation. Not flexibility. It's the practice of feeling how every part of the system relates to every other part and finding the balance where coherence emerges across the whole.

When the topology is balanced, the experience at every node is one of forming. Change flows coherently. Dissolution serves formation everywhere. The system is alive — fully in motion, fully in change — and the path is good.

When the topology stabilizes in an imbalanced state, something interesting happens: neither side thrives. Resources pooling here, deprivation locked in there — and every node feels the incoherence, because every node is coupled to the topology. There are no walls in information structure. The wealthy in a polarized society aren't comfortable with a side of guilt. They're living inside broken resolution dynamics and they likely feel it in the actual structure of their experience, whether or not they can name it.

Polarization, in this view, isn't disagreement. It's the topology stabilizing in a state where imbalance is self-reinforcing. Attractor basins deepening on both sides. Hysteresis increasing until the walls between regions become vertical. Nobody can transition. Nobody is skiing. Everyone is in a slow avalanche and calling it identity.

World peace, then, wouldn't be the absence of conflict. It wouldn't be stability — stability is stasis, and stasis is nonexistence. World peace would be coherence: an information topology where every node contributes to its neighbors, where dissolution and formation flow together, where the inevitable discomfort of change has a clear path toward new resolution, and where the experience of navigating that flow is one of aliveness.

The product isn't a policy or an argument or a technology. The product is the experience of coherence itself — designing environments, interactions, and information topologies where balance isn't imposed but felt. Where every part of the system can sense its contribution to the whole. Where change moves like a body in motion, not like a mountain collapsing.

If this framework is right, this is what the brain does — correlates phase across an information topology. This is how experience defines good and suffering. And this might be how you approach world peace: not by convincing anyone of anything, but by shaping the topology so that coherence is the natural path through the state space of shared experience.
