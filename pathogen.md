## The Network Model

A distributed network defined not by infrastructure but by a cryptographic trust chain. A root keypair defines the network — membership is established by signing keys down from that root. The trust chain is auditable and clippable — compromised nodes can be revoked without affecting the rest.

Addressing and routing emerge from the same graph. Services are provided and required by nodes. A gateway is just anything that provides `list` — a directory of what the network can reach. The whole system reduces to four primitives:

- **Request** — I need something
- **Provide** — I have something to offer
- **List** — what's available
- **Audit** — what happened, verify the chain

The network is a dependency graph. Trust, routing, access control, and discovery are all just views of that graph.

## Identity and Access

(Should also include multipath encryption. If all data is distributed across multiple nodes, its resileint against encryption breaks because the full data set is not available to hack. Meaning even quantum systems can't break the encryption because the data is physically not there. You need to compromise at least 2 systems of a known set).

Membership is defined by keys, not usernames or accounts. Onboarding a team member means handing them a keypair and a gateway into the project network. Offboarding means clipping that gateway from the chain. No accounts to delete, no permissions to audit.

Access is a relationship between networks, not a permission granted to a person. A team member adds the project to their personal network. The project gets a gateway back. The relationship is bidirectional, auditable, and scoped.

Software becomes a resource in the graph — it runs wherever the trust chain allows, moves between networks, and the only thing that matters is the chain. This separates who manages, who owns, and who uses. A remote system can provision a network, hand it off to a local station, have it managed by an entirely separate entity — all transparently.

## Public Identity and the Social Graph

A public trust chain — a minimal set of verified keys with little attached data. Your key is your identity. Institutions attest against it — the bank records that this key belongs to a verified member, an employer records employment. Attestations are scoped, revocable, and don't reveal each other.

Different keys expose different surfaces. The public sees a minimal anchor. Friends see more. Institutions see what's relevant to them. Your data sits in distributed devices and is served through whatever gateways you've opened. No platform owns it. No vendor controls the relationship.

This is the social network as it should work. The social graph is just the dependency graph. Culture is people sharing what they've lived, not content optimized for engagement.

## Bus Stops as Community Hubs

The bus stop is already the commons — where people wait, where cities invest, where the least resourced people already are. Redesigned as a community hub:

- Solar canopy provides shade and power generation
- Device charging, later scooter/bike charging
- Free internet access with a landing page of support services — food banks, employment, housing, legal aid
- Physical address provision through volunteers for court documents and employment
- Entry point to the broader network

The scooter charging ties into existing transit infrastructure. Government approval is an attestation in the trust chain, not operational involvement. If government won't attest, any trusted body can — adding competition to the commons.

## Education Without Age Segregation

Schools as buildings that provide education services, not age-sorted institutions. Learning happens at whatever level you're at, when you need it. Experienced people teach naturally. Older students tutor younger ones as real work. Childcare, education, and employment occupy the same space.

Kids integrated into real community life learn it by living it. The industrial transition cliff — eighteen years of structure then nothing — dissolves. Traffic concentrations dissolve with it, since resources aren't concentrated by age group in single locations.

## Distributed Welfare and Labor

A distributed welfare system removes the perverse incentives of centralized programs — the work penalty, cliff effects, means testing. Support tapers continuously rather than cutting off. A child's needs are met based on the child's needs, not routed through parental eligibility. Different communities can run different models without federal mandate.

Consumer and worker coalitions built on mutual support have no legal surface to attack. No union to decertify, no strike to break. Just people taking care of each other who happen to work at the same place.

A browser layer shows the real cost of goods when workers are paid fairly — not a boycott, just information at the point of decision. A fund routes a portion of purchases to offset worker wages. Consumers opt in. Amazon can't opt out of their workers needing a living wage.

## Exchange and Trade

A literal exchange program — house and job swaps across communities and borders. You slot into another network with your skills, they provide what the commons provides. Someone from Haiti visiting Detroit brings context that doesn't exist there and takes back connections that don't exist at home. Both communities are richer.

Borders become points of interest rather than boundaries. The trust chain already establishes that you're a legitimate participant — no corporate visa sponsorship required.

This makes supply chains human. When you've lived in the place your coffee comes from, the trade relationship is something you have a stake in getting right. Communities that exchange people and knowledge are harder to manipulate into conflict. The authoritarian move requires an enemy — harder to make one of somewhere you've been.

## On Technology and AI

AI is useful for the parts that aren't the point — logistics, translation, routing, paperwork. The work itself — building trust, connecting people, figuring out what a community needs — is human and stays human.

The tech is interesting because the problem is interesting, not because the problem was invented to need the tech. You don't need AI to make the work feel meaningful because the work is meaningful.

A story is worth hearing because someone lived something and found a way to share it. That's irreplaceable.

## The Starting Point

It's buildable because it doesn't require anything that doesn't already exist. Each step is immediately useful — not an investment in future reward, but realized progress. It can grow at whatever pace a community can sustain, pause when things are hard, resume when they stabilize.

It doesn't ask permission. It starts where people are, with what exists, and grows by being useful. The institution either adapts or becomes irrelevant.

Someone sitting at a bus stop with a QR code can receive a meal ordered by someone across the world who wanted to do something real with ten dollars. That's the whole thing in miniature — someone noticed a problem and did what they could.
